Insurability Implications Of Tooth Loss

Submitted by hankgeorge on Wednesday, June 15, 2011 - 14:41.

This paper examines evidence in the clinical literature supporting the assumption that there are significant mortality and morbidity implications associated with tooth loss at older ages.

Readers will hopefully come away impressed with the value of asking about tooth loss on teleinterviews, especially at ages 55 and older.

What are the medical synonyms for "toothless"?

Edentulous, edentate and edentulism.

You will see these terms used in two contexts: complete toothlessness as well as the loss of a substantial percentage of one's teeth.

How common is edentulism? [Polzer, Musacchio]

Country Age Groups % Edentulous
Australia ≥ 60 (urban only) 25%
New Zealand 65-74 59%
UK ≥ 65 46%
USA ≥ 65 41%
Canada ≥ 65 58%
Italy ≥ 65 44%

In a 1999 survey of Americans aged 65-74, 13% with incomes between $35,000 and $50,000 were toothless; even when their income was $50,000 or higher, nearly 8% were nevertheless bereft of natural teeth! [Bell]

We may therefore conclude that the loss of many or all teeth is highly prevalent in the elderly.

What are the major risk factors for significant tooth loss? [Hanioka, Hung, Kaumudi, Musacchio, Ojima, Shultis, Spahr]

  • The #1 risk factor is periodontal disease
  • Cigarette smoking, especially pack-years of exposure
  • Diabetes
  • Low serum albumin — a major marker for impaired nutrition

Neither level of alcohol use nor obesity appears to be a significant predictor. [Hanioka and Ojima]

What is periodontal disease?

Gum disease, divided into two main subsets: gingivitis and periodontitis.

Gingivitis is related to dental plaque causing redness, swelling and bleeding. It is the precursor of periodontitis if not treated.

Periodontitis is present when gum infection spreads into the deeper tissues surrounding the teeth, leading to breakdown of gingival tissues and bone loss.

How does edentulism specifically impact diabetics?

In one study, the glycosylated hemoglobin (HbA1-c) levels in edentulous subjects averaged 9.1%, as compared to 6.1% in those who had all of their teeth.

Furthermore, the risks of microalbuminuria and progression to end stage renal disease were 2.6- and 4.9-fold greater, respectively. [Shultis]

Is heart disease more prevalent when edentulism is present?

Yes:

  • In a study of 3856 subjects ages 60 and over, 28% who were edentulous had cardiac disease as compared to just 11% who had their teeth. [Yu]
  • In an analysis of 5 clinical studies encompassing over 86,000 subjects followed for an average of 6 years, the relative risk of developing heart disease was 25% greater in the edentulous cohort. [Bahekar]
  • A 24-year follow-up of 1203 patients in a veteran's care facility reported a 1.6-fold higher likelihood of coronary disease in edentate individuals. [Dietrich]
  • Another study followed 2900 persons for 7.5 years, matching those with no natural teeth to controls who had most of their teeth. The edentulous group was at a 50% higher risk of developing cardiovascular disease. [Heitmann]

Völzke and coworkers assessed the risk of aortic valve sclerosis (AVS) in 2341 subjects ages 45 and over. As underwriters know, this disorder is associated with atherosclerosis and puts the patient at risk for aortic stenosis.

They discovered the following relationship between number of teeth lost and risk of AVS:

Number of Missing Teeth Observed Risk
≤ 5 1.00
5-10 1.4
11-18 1.8
19-27 1.4
≥ 28 2.0

Clearly, edentulism is a significant predictor of increased risk of cardiovascular disease.

Is edentulism also implicated in other circulatory diseases?

Yes.

  • The risk of carotid artery stenosis is > 2-fold increased. [Schillinger]
  • The likelihood of a stroke is 40% greater. [Wu]
  • The risk of peripheral arterial disease is also 40% higher. [Hung]

Is there any correlation between tooth loss and cognitive function?

Yes.

A 2010 study found that higher rates of tooth loss independently predicted an increased risk of low cognitive test scores. For each tooth lost per decade, the likelihood of a lower MMSE increased between 9% and 12%. This result did not change after adjusting for smoking, diabetes or other likely confounders. [Kaye]

An earlier investigation revealed that elderly women with 9 or fewer teeth had twice the probability of being diagnosed with dementia as women who had at least 10 teeth. [Gatz]

One researcher reported that tooth loss was insignificant in this context after adjusting for IQ…but this adjustment, alas, is beyond the scope of underwriting inquiry! [Starr]

Does this risk extend to functional impairment as well?

It does.

A brand new study showed that edentate subjects had a 60% greater incidence of Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADL) impairment, as well as a 30% higher rate of lower extremity mobility problems. [Yu]

There is also a direct correlation between tooth loss and 10-year risk of disability in septuagenarians: [Holm-Pedersen]

Number of Teeth at Baseline Relative Risk of Disability
20 or more 1.0
10-19 1.5
1-9 3.0
None 1.9

Some actuary will no doubt conclude that if you have fewer than 10 teeth left, you might as well have the remaining ones yanked out!

The point, however, is that losing essentially 50% or more of one's teeth directly correlates with the odds of late life disability.

Is there any association between being edentulous and the risk of cancer?

There is, at least for carcinomas of the head and neck area:

  • Garrote (Cuba) showed that the risk of these tumors increased nearly 3-fold when subjects had lost at least half of their natural teeth.
  • A second study discovered nearly the same degree of oral malignancy risk in persons missing between 8 and 15 teeth. [Guha]
  • A review of 6 studies pegged this risk at between 2- and 3-fold overall. [Meyer]

Have any other disorders been convincingly associated with significant tooth loss?

Yes — osteoporosis. [Bos]

Have there been any assessments of the impact of tooth loss on mortality?

We found 4 studies, all done within the last 5 years:

  • 2006: In a Japanese investigation involving octogenarians, each missing tooth increased 4-year mortality by 4.2%. [Ansai]
  • 2008: In a similar study of septuagenarian Swedes, each remaining tooth reduced the 7-year risk of death by 4%. [Osterberg]
  • 2009: After following 41,000 adults for 16 years, these epidemiologists found that mortality was 10% in those who had at least some teeth vs. 19% in those who were completely toothless. [Brown]
  • 2010: Another Swedish assessment which evaluated 7674 individuals after 12 years documented a 7-fold increase in mortality in those who had < 10 teeth when they were compared to subjects possessing > 25 of their natural teeth. [Holmlund]

Taken together, these reports make a compelling case for significant extra mortality in applicants who are largely or wholly edentulous.

Are chewing problems also associated with insurability?

Yes.

For example, a Japanese investigation found that individuals meeting their criteria for "significantly impaired chewing" had over 4.5 times greater risk of cardiovascular disease, as compared to those with unencumbered mastication. [Ansai and Takata]

An Italian project looked at 1-year mortality in 2755 elders, mean age 82, and discovered that even after adjusting for unintended weight loss and cognitive dysfunction, mortality was 50% higher when subjects reported they had difficulties chewing normal foods. [Onder]

What are the risk factors associated with impaired chewing capacity?

The Italian researchers [Onder] identified 6 factors disproportionately prevalent in those with masticatory problems:

  1. Impaired Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)
  2. Cognitive dysfunction
  3. Unintended weight loss
  4. Coronary disease
  5. Peripheral arterial disease
  6. Depression

By inference, one concludes that in addition to substantial excess short-duration mortality, individuals with chewing problems are also at longer-term risk for mortality and morbidity.

Are there specific RED FLAGS at ages 70 and over that work synergistically with extensive tooth loss and chewing problems to insidiously increase mortality and morbidity?

In our opinion, there are at least 6:

  1. Severe, untreated periodontitis
  2. Unintentional weight loss > 5% of pre-loss weight
  3. 50 or more pack-years of cigarette smoking, whether or not the individual continues to smoke
  4. Any degree of low serum albumin
  5. Markedly low cholesterol (< 120 mg/dL or < 3.11 mmol/L)
  6. ALT and AST readings < 8 U/L

The bottom line here is that we need to make note of comments in medical records related to both extensive tooth loss and chewing problems reported by elderly applicants, and also consider asking about tooth loss on geriatric teleinterviews.

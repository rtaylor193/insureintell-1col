cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CLE) http://insureintell.github.io/ en Cutaneous Lupus Erythematosus http://insureintell.github.io/node/25655 <div data-history-node-id="25655" class="layout layout--onecol"> <div class="layout__region layout__region--content"> <div> <div>Body</div> <div property="schema:text"><p><strong>Note to Readers</strong></p> <p>This article supplements the content of our 2007 CE course on systemic lupus (SLE), where we did not have enough room to address this important topic.</p> <p> </p> <p><strong>Why is this subject worth reading about?</strong></p> <p>Most underwriters know a great deal more about SLE (systemic lupus erythematosus) than they do about “skin lupus.”</p> <p>The intention of this article is to raise awareness of the underwriting issues in the main forms of skin lupus.</p> <!--break--><!--break--> <p><strong>Is it fair to say that skin lupus and systemic lupus are the same disease?</strong></p> <p>Yes – the lupus is just manifesting differently.</p> <p>Some patients get skin disease only, whereas others get both.</p> <p>The “why” of this fact remains to be definitively established. [Bano]</p> <p><strong>What are the 3 main types of cutaneous (skin) lupus?</strong></p> <ul> <li>Chronic cutaneous lupus erythematosus (CCLE), of which the only type encountered more<br /> than rarely is discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE)</li> <li>Subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE)</li> <li>Acute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (ACLE)</li> </ul> <p><strong>Does cutaneous lupus always mean skin only? </strong></p> <blockquote> <p><em>“The term cutaneous lupus erythematosus is applied to patients with skin lesions caused by lupus erythematosus, regardless of whether the disease is confined to the skin or represents part of a more generalized systemic disease process.”</em></p> <blockquote> <p>Dimitrios T. Boumpas, MD, et al.<br /> <br /> <em>Annals of Internal Medicine</em><br /> <br /> 123(1995):42</p> </blockquote> </blockquote> <p>In other words, just being told it is “cutaneous” or “skin” lupus, without knowing the precise type, can mislead one into thinking a case of SLE with skin involvement is a case of skin lupus only!</p> <p><em>Know the precise type every time!</em></p> <p><strong>How common is skin involvement in systemic lupus (SLE)?</strong> [Bano]</p> <ul> <li>58% have a malar (butterfly) rash – more on this later</li> <li>45% have photosensitivity (adverse response to direct sunlight)</li> <li>10% will have “discoid lesions”</li> <li>6% will have subacute cutaneous LE (SCLE)</li> </ul> <p><strong>When does skin lupus typically arise?</strong></p> <p>Ages 20-40, but it can arise at any age. [Tebbe]</p> <p><strong>Is it more common in women?</strong></p> <p>Yes, roughly 3-4:1 in DLE (discoid) and 3-6:1 in SCLE. [Hackett, Tebbe]</p> <p><strong>What can trigger the onset of skin lupus? [Rothfield] </strong></p> <ul> <li>Exposure to sunlight, cold and x-ray radiation</li> <li>Trauma to the skin</li> <li>Skin infection</li> <li>Chemical burns</li> <li>Various kinds of dermatitis</li> <li>Scars</li> </ul> <p><strong>Is it significant when a proposed insured tells us he/she must wear sunblock and/or minimize sunlight exposure in some other manner on a regular basis? </strong></p> <p>Absolutely.</p> <p>Lupus is just one of a number of possible reasons with underwriting implications. This comment always needs to be pursued.</p> <p><strong>How is sun protection achieved in all cases of lupus?</strong></p> <ul> <li>Avoid direct sun exposure</li> <li>Sunscreens of higher potency</li> <li>Tightly woven clothing, broad-brimmed hats</li> </ul> <p><strong>Is failure to adhere to these recommendations significant?</strong></p> <p>Yes, for two reasons:</p> <ul> <li>It will usually result in exacerbations/worsening of skin lesions.</li> <li>It speaks to a deeper issue of risk-taking in the form of non-adherence to medical recommendations, which many feel is a marker for other adverse behaviors…and there is plenty of evidence now to support this belief!</li> </ul> <p><strong>In what two ways has the term “discoid lupus” come to be used clinically?</strong></p> <ol> <li>To designate a subset of persons with skin disease only and no significant evidence of systemic involvement (which is how we tend to use the term).</li> <li>To refer generically to a number of distinctive skin lesions in SLE (which, for the record, are<br /> rarely associated with aggressive forms of SLE).</li> </ol> <p>On balance, when the term “discoid lupus” is used, the other facts disclosed should be consistent with DLE… and NOT with SCLE, ACLE or overt SLE.</p> <p><strong>Where do discoid skin lesions usually occur?</strong></p> <ul> <li>They can occur anywhere but most involve the scalp, face and/or ears.</li> <li>Scalp lesions occur in 60% and can be the only presentation in 10%.</li> <li>The V-region of the neck is also involved in a minority of cases.</li> <li>Mucosal DLE is uncommon; it may involve the mouth, nasal passages, conjunctiva and even genital mucosa. [Rothfield]</li> <li>Lesions below the neck are distinctly uncommon and most that do occur are on the extensor surfaces of the arms.</li> <li>When other lesions below the neck are present, the diagnosis of DLE should be questioned unless it is clearly established by biopsy.</li> <li>Patients with lesions below the neck will sometimes be said to have “widespread DLE.” [Callen, 2005]</li> </ul> <p><strong>Is the “butterfly rash” a feature of DLE? </strong></p> <p>No – this is a RED FLAG for ACLE and systemic disease.</p> <p><strong>Is cigarette smoking a risk factor for SLE? </strong></p> <p>Big time! [Miot]</p> <ul> <li>The risk is almost 3-fold higher</li> <li>Cumulative past exposure is less of an issue than current use</li> <li>Smokers tend to have more extensive disease at onset</li> </ul> <p><strong>Do DLE patients ever have positive lupus-related autoantibody tests? </strong></p> <p>Most cases do not have a positive ANA or, if they do, only a minimally increased titer in the range of 1:40. [Sontheimer, 1997]</p> <p>Any time the titer is higher or other autoantibodies are present, question the presumed diagnosis of DLE – <strong><em>it is probably wrong! </em></strong></p> <p><strong>Is a biopsy always needed to diagnose DLE? </strong></p> <p>No…but it is the only way to absolutely confirm the Dx and rule out other possible causes of the suspicious lesion. [Callen, 2006]</p> <p><strong>What % with ostensible DLE progress to SLE? </strong></p> <p>< 7%, and most of these will be misdiagnosed cases of SCLE and/or have multiple persistent laboratory abnormalities. [Millard]</p> <p><strong>What is the classic lesion of acute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (ACLE)?</strong></p> <p>The “butterfly rash.” [Callen, 2005]</p> <p><strong>Is ACLE usually associated with systemic disease? </strong></p> <p>Yes – and more often than not, relatively severe systemic disease! [Cullen, 2006]</p> <p><strong>How does subacute cutaneous lupus erythematosus (SCLE) differ from SLE and ACLE? </strong></p> <p>It is more extensive than SLE, typically involving the upper back and usually spares the upper neck and face.</p> <p>SCLE patients have chronically recurring skin disease and also often have arthralgias, malaise and other generalized symptoms which do not occur in SLE.</p> <p>10% of SCLE patients have central nervous system and renal disease, and for all intents and purposes represent severe systemic disease.</p> <p><strong>Is SCLE more likely to have positive autoantibody tests than DLE?</strong></p> <p>Yes, significantly so, with 80% having an ANA positive with titer of 1:160 or higher (which is in the SLE range). Other autoantibodies associated with systemic disease are also frequently found as well. [Sontheimer, 1985]</p> <p><strong>Does Rx cause SCLE? </strong></p> <p>Yes, there is a huge list of drugs which can induce SCLE. None do so more than rarely.</p> <p><strong><em>ACLE and SCLE must never be confused with DLE </em></strong><em>– this is one of the two main issues involved in underwriting skin lupus, the other being to rule out systemic disease (SLE). </em></p> <p><strong>Why is skin lupus treated?</strong></p> <ul> <li>To improve the patient’s physical appearance</li> <li>To prevent scarring, skin atrophy or pigmentation changes</li> </ul> <p><strong>How are most cases of DLE treated? </strong></p> <ul> <li>Topic hydrocortisone.</li> <li>Intralesional injection of a steroid in very localized DLE.</li> <li>If these do not work, hydroxychloroquine. However, hydroxychloroquine may also be used in the other forms of skin lupus as well as in SLE.</li> </ul> <p><strong>What other drugs are used to treat more severe DLE as well as SCLE? </strong></p> <ul> <li>Retinoids such as isotretinoin.</li> <li>Tacrolimus and pimecrolimus.</li> <li>Thalidomide – which may be the best drug for severe cases of skin lupus; it is also used in<br /> SLE. [Cuadrado]</li> <li>Chloroquine and quinacrine.</li> <li>When immunosuppressives, biological agents and other SLE drugs are prescribed, you<br /> can safely bet that systemic disease is present.</li> </ul> <p><strong>Do most patients with DLE get a remission?</strong></p> <p>If they have only head and neck disease, yes, at least 50% remit, often without recurrence of obvious/active skin lesions for many years, even decades.</p> <p>However, if the disease is more widespread, then < 10% become clinically inactive despite Rx. [Callen, 2006]</p> <p><strong>Is there any extra mortality in DLE? </strong></p> <blockquote> <p><em>“Discoid lupus is a chronic indolent skin disorder with no significant extra mortality.” </em></p> <blockquote> <p>David S. Williams, MD<br /> <br /> Ohio National Financial Services<br /> <br /> <em>Journal of Insurance Medicine </em><br /> <br /> 37(2005):70</p> </blockquote> </blockquote> <p><strong>What is the mortality issue in DLE? </strong></p> <p>Making certain it is discoid only…as opposed to SCLE, ACLE or DLE.</p> <p><strong>A</strong><strong>re there disability implications in DLE?</strong></p> <p>In a German study, 45% of patients with DLE experienced “vocational handicaps,” although one would think that in those with less extensive disease this would be minimal, except perhaps in some public contact jobs. [Meller]</p> <p><strong>Are treatment costs higher in SLE than in DLE?</strong></p> <p>Absolutely.</p> <p>SLE treatment cost 12-fold more dollars in one study, once the clinical workup is done and SLE is ruled out.</p> <p>Most of the medication used in DLE is topical and relatively cheap, compared to other drugs used in SLE.</p> <p>Inpatient care is rare (< 3%) in DLE. Periodic visits to a community dermatologist constitute most of the care after the diagnosis is made. [Hackett]</p> <p><strong>Is there a “WORST CASE” for DLE? </strong></p> <p>Not really (at least for mortality). However, there are scenarios which need to be carefully<br /> underwritten:</p> <ul> <li>Diagnosis is uncertain, especially if disease is more extensive than just on the face, scalp and/or ears (raising a question of misdiagnosis of SCLE).</li> <li>Inadequate autoantibody testing, defined as not having at least an ANA test.</li> <li>Persistently positive ANA ≥1:50 with a speckled or homogeneous immunofluorescent pattern. [Millard]</li> <li>Presence of leukopenia or thrombocytopenia not otherwise explained.</li> <li>With symptoms or other findings associated with systemic disease.</li> </ul> <p><strong>What about the other kinds of skin lupus? </strong></p> <ul> <li>ACLE should be assumed to be associated (either concurrently or eventually) with a diagnosis of systemic disease.</li> <li>SCLE has a considerable risk of progressing to systemic disease.</li> <li>One would think SCLE rarely acceptable “standard” (unless it has been confined to the skin for many years with consistently favorable autoantibody tests).</li> </ul> <p><strong>Do DLE and SLE ever arise at the same time? </strong></p> <p>Yes, rarely…and when they do, most studies show that the SLE tends to be rather mild, without lethal renal disease. [Callen, 2005]</p> <p> </p> <p><strong>References</strong></p> <p>Bano. <em>Clinical and Experimental Rheumatology.</em> 24, Supplement 40(2006):S26<br /> Callen, 2005. <em>Best Practice and Research Clinical Rheumatology.</em> 19(2005):767<br /> Callen, 2006. <em>Australian Journal of Dermatology.</em> 47(2006):13<br /> Cuadrado. <em>American Journal of Medicine.</em> 177(2005):246<br /> Hackett. <em>Arthritis & Rheumatism. </em>52, Supplement(September, 2005):S36<br /> Meller. <em>Autoimmune Review.</em> 4(2005):242<br /> Millard. <em>Archives of Dermatology.</em> 115(1979):1055<br /> Miot. <em>Dermatology.</em> 211(2006):118<br /> Rothfield. <em>Clinics in Dermatology. </em>24(2006):348<br /> Sontheimer, 1977. <em>Lupus.</em> 6(1997):84<br /> Sontheimer, 1985. <em>The Journal of Dermatology. </em>12(1985):20</p> </div> </div> </div> </div> Thu, 01 May 2008 04:00:00 +0000 hankgeorge 25655 at http://insureintell.github.io

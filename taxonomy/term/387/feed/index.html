cutting http://insureintell.github.io/ en Underwriting Implications of Intentional Self-Harm http://insureintell.github.io/node/25713 <div data-history-node-id="25713" class="layout layout--onecol"> <div class="layout__region layout__region--content"> <div> <div>Body</div> <div property="schema:text"><p style="padding-top: 2em"><em>“Today, there is, across the world, a sharp public awareness of self-harm as a major mental health issue.”</em></p> <p>Sander L. Gilman, PhD<br /> Emory University Medical School<br /> Journal of Mental and Nervous Disease<br /> 200(2012):1008</p> <p><em>“Self-harm is a common clinical problem but is poorly understood and arouses ambivalent feelings in health professionals.”</em></p> <p>Keren Skegg, MD<br /> University of Otaga Medical School, New Zealand<br /> Lancet<br /> 366(2005):1471</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What is deliberate self-harm (DHS)?</strong></p> <p>As is so often the case in psychiatric terminology, this gets a bit confusing.</p> <p>There are 3 terms used by psychiatrists in this context: [Andover]</p> <ul> <li>Deliberate self-harm</li> <li>Non-fatal self-injury</li> <li>Non-suicidal self-injury</li> </ul> <p>“Non-suicidal self-injury” (NSII) <em>excludes</em> all acts known or strongly suspected to be suicide attempts.</p> <p>“Non-fatal self-injury” (NFSI) and “deliberate self harm” (DSH) include <em>all</em> purposeful self-injuries, regardless of intent. Indeed, some cases will be referred to as suicidal gestures (para-suicides) or suicide attempts.</p> <p>Because suicide gestures and attempts are relatively more ominous from an underwriting perspective, we will use yet another term – intentional self-harm (ISH) – to encompass all acts of deliberate self-harm <em>except</em> overt suicide gestures and attempts.</p> <p>And we will also examine the links between ISH, overt suicidal gestures/attempts and completed suicides.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is intentional self-harm included in the new DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th edition) as a formal diagnostic entity?</strong></p> <p>No.</p> <p>The draft version did include a self-harm disorder category with criteria to make the diagnosis. [Gilman] However, when DSM-5 was published (May 2013), self-harm per se was not included as a formal diagnosis and only minor mention was made of it under “other conditions that may be a focus of clinical attention” (which translates as: potential diagnostic entities for future consideration).</p> <p>There are 2 formal diagnoses in DSM-V that involve quite specific forms of intentional self-harm in DSM-V:</p> <ul> <li>Trichotillomania (compulsive pulling of one’s own hair)</li> <li>Excoriation disorder (compulsive skin picking and hopefully one’s own as well!)</li> </ul> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What are the more prevalent forms of ISH?</strong></p> <p>The 2 most common are self-poisoning, almost always with medicinal products and self-cutting. [Bethell, Hawton]</p> <p>Others include burning, scratching, gouging, carving words or symbols into skin, self-hitting, hair pulling, stopping medication to cause self-harm and deliberate recklessness.[Skegg]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>How many episodes of intentional self-harm present for medical care annually?</strong></p> <p>650,000 cases in the United States…and no doubt the substantial majority of episodes do not lead to care seeking by the self-harming subject. [Cooper]</p> <p><em>Self-harm is far more common than most of us realize.</em></p> <p>The prevalences in UK and Hong Kong adolescents were 16.5% and 23.5%, respectively. [Law, Lereya]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>At what ages is self-harm most common?</strong></p> <p>The most common age for first episode is 16 years old and the overall risk is several-fold higher at ages 18-30 vs. older ages.</p> <p>However, self-harm occurs at all ages and when it first appears over age 50, the risk of suicide is higher than at younger ages.[O’Connor, Skegg]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What are <span style="color: red; font-weight: bold;">RED FLAGS</span> for intentional self-harm?</strong></p> <ul> <li>Prior episode is highly predictive of recurring events</li> <li>Risk-taking behaviors including heavy smoking</li> <li>History of childhood sexual or physical abuse</li> <li>Prior suicidal ideation</li> <li>Substance abuse of all types</li> <li>Multiple episodes of major depression or bipolar disorder, especially with anhedonia or hopelessness as prominent features</li> <li>Post-partum depression is a particularly common depression context for ISH.</li> <li>Conduct, oppositional defiant and ADHD diagnoses in children and adolescents</li> <li>High level of anger as a response to life events</li> <li>Being bullied in childhood</li> <li>Longstanding or frequently-recurring benzodiazepine use</li> <li>Chronic severe insomnia and frequent nightmares</li> <li>Social isolation</li> </ul> <p>[Andover, Challis, Healey, Lereya, Moller, Shih, Singareddy, Wisner, Wu]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Where are we most likely to find undisclosed histories of intentional self-harm?</strong></p> <p>Emergency care visits for reasons like cuts, “accidental” poisoning, repeat episodes of injury/accident, etc. [Kuehl, Laukkanen]</p> <p>In a London study, 48% of patients seen for penetrating neck wounds sustained the injury due to self-harm. [Siau]</p> <p>Patients seeking emergency care for self-harm who are admitted as inpatients have a high risk of using more lethal attempts as well as a prior diagnosis of a major psychiatric disorder. [Olfson]</p> <p><em>Applicants with <span style="color: red; font-weight: bold;">RED FLAG </span> risk factors who go to the emergency department for these reasons should be underwritten carefully, in many cases with emergency care records.</em></p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Are repeat visits due to self-harm common?</strong></p> <p>Yes.</p> <p>In one study, 18% had a second episode of emergency care for this reason within 12 months of the first event. Over 7 years, 53% had two or more self-harm events needing immediate intervention. [Hawton]</p> <p>In another investigation, 24.6% had a second visit in the near-term future. [Bilen]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Do most patients seeking emergency care for ISH have a subsequent psychiatric assessment?</strong></p> <p>No.</p> <p>In a large US study, roughly 50% were referred on for such assessments. [Olfson]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>When is self-cutting strongly associated with major psychiatric illness?</strong></p> <p>When it involves sites other than the arm [Laukkanen]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Are nonsuicidal self-harm episodes predictive of future suicidal ideation, attempts and completed suicides?</strong></p> <p>Absolutely.</p> <p>Experts believe that self-harm is actually an early manifestation of suicidality that often progresses to these other stages. [Brausch, Cox]</p> <p>At least 40% of suicide completers have a prior history of self-harm. [Nandi]</p> <p>Whitlock reported that intentional self-harm increased the likelihood of a subsequent suicide attempt 3-fold.</p> <p>In an earlier study, Whitlock and Knox showed that the likelihood of suicide plans and gestures increased 6-fold and 7-fold respectively in patients treated initially for nonsuicidal self-harm episodes.</p> <p>Over 20 years of followup, subjects with just a single episode of intentional self-harm had a 4-fold higher risk of suicide when compared to persons with no self-injury history. [Jenkins]</p> <p>In another study, 5% of self-harm patients committed suicide within 9 years. [Owens]</p> <p>The highest risk interval from intentional self-harm to suicide is within 12 months of seeking emergency care. [Cooper]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is the suicide risk in self-harmers mainly due to comorbid psychiatric disorders?</strong></p> <p>No – the risk is independent of that conferred by mental illness. [Guan, Madsen]</p> <p>A new study showed that intentional self-harm doubled the risk of suicide in patients with affective (mood) disorders. [Nordentoft]</p> <p>Therefore, in someone with a psychiatric condition at substantially heightened risk of suicide, episodes of intentional self-harm significantly increase the probability of suicide.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What are the <span style="color: red; font-weight: bold;">RED FLAGS</span> for suicide in persons with a history of intentional self-harm?</strong></p> <ul> <li>Past or current major psychiatric illness</li> <li>Medically severe self-injuries</li> <li>Self-harm events using methods similar to those used in suicide attempts</li> <li>Self-harm by partial asphyxiation</li> <li>Premeditated vs. spontaneous self-injuries</li> <li>Traffic-related intentional self-harm</li> <li>Denying at the time of emergency care that an obvious self-injury was intentional</li> <li>Clues to current/recent substance abuse in the medical history, driving record, on lab tests, etc.</li> <li>Poor physical health, mainly at older ages</li> <li>Unemployed</li> <li>Legal problems</li> <li>Living alone with social isolation</li> </ul> <p>[Bergen and Hawton, Cooper, Chen, Harriss, Miller, Silverman, Skegg]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is there any extra mortality risk in intentional self-harm?</strong></p> <p>Yes.</p> <p>Studies have reported that persons with a self-harm history had 2-fold greater all-cause mortality. [Carter, Hawton and Harriss, Neeleman]</p> <p>Bergen assessed 30,950 individuals who presented at one of 6 hospitals with intentional self-injuries and were followed for a median interval of 6 years thereafter.</p> <ul> <li>6% died over this interval</li> <li>Mean age at death was 49.6 years old in males and 54.3 in women.</li> <li>1 in 3 deaths were due to accidents and suicides.</li> <li>Deaths in this population due to both natural and unnatural causes were greater than expected.</li> </ul> <h1 style="padding-top: 1em">What are the 6 take-home messages for underwriters?</h1> <ol> <li>Intentional self-harm is common</li> <li>Most cases are not detected during the underwriting process; nondisclosure and vague histories assumed to be due to accidents are the likely drivers of this low rate of case recognition during the risk appraisal process.</li> <li>Intentional self-harm is a marker for a high risk of psychiatric illness.</li> <li>Intentional self-harm is a major risk factor for suicidal ideation, suicidal gestures and attempts, and completed suicides.</li> <li>Intentional self-harm is associated with significant excess mortality and morbidity.</li> <li>Underwriters need to be alert to the <span style="color: red; font-weight: bold;">RED FLAGS</span> and other clues to intentional self-harm, as well as to the increased suicide risk in persons with a history of self-harm.</li> </ol> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>References</strong></p> <p>Andover.

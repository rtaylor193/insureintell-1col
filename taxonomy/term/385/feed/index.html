Mayo, BM, BCh<br /> Oxford University<br /> Psychiatric Clinics of North America<br /> 25(2002):27</p> <p><em>"Although alcohol-related fatal crashes were substantially reduced between 1982 and 1995, there has been little change over the last 10 years."</em></p> <p>James D. Fell, et al<br /> Pacific Institute for Research and Evaluation<br /> Annals of Advances in Automotive Medicine<br /> 53(2009):63</p> <p>Traffic crashes are the leading cause of death at ages 2 to 33 in the USA and many other western countries. [Hingson]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>How common is driving under the influence?</strong></p> <ul> <li>In a 2011 survey, 1.8% of American adults admitted to driving drunk at least once in the prior year. This equates to roughly 4 million people. [MMWR, 2011]</li> <li>Epidemiologic calculations suggest that there were 112 million drunken driving events in 2010, which is down 31% from the prior year's estimate. [MMWR, 2011]</li> <li>In the USA, 1.4 million motorists are arrested (and in most cases, convicted) for driving while intoxicated (DWI). [Furr-Holden]</li> <li>The odds of a person with a BAC (blood alcohol content) of 0.10 being arrested for DWI on a given driving episode is estimated at no more than 1 in 200. [Beitel]</li> <li>Roughly 1/3rd of all US motor vehicle crash fatalities involved drivers with a blood alcohol content of at least 0.08, consistent with unequivocal intoxication. [MMWR, 2011]</li> <li>In a new study of 16,942 fatal crashes, 40% of fatally injured drivers had a BAC of 0.08 or higher. [Romano]</li> <li>In another new study, this one with 20,150 fatal car accidents, 40% of drivers killed in these accidents were intoxicated. [Brady]</li> <li>In Canada, 28% of fatally injured drivers at ages 26-35 had BAC concentrations at or above the same threshold. [Maskalyk]</li> <li>20% of serious auto accidents in urban China are attributed to alcohol excess and this number is thought to be an understatement due to imperfect record keeping. [Li, Rao]</li> <li>In Ireland, 30% of drivers in fatal crashes were intoxicated. [Bedford]</li> <li>In Australia, it was 29% [Drummer]</li> <li>Only 14% of drunken drivers in American fatal crashes have an existing diagnosis of alcohol dependency; therefore, the vast majority will not have a clinical history of an alcohol use disorder. [Furr-Holden]</li> </ul> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What about intoxicated use of a motorcycle?</strong></p> <p><em>"…drinking and riding a motorcycle is a stubborn problem in which total alcohol-related crashes are increasing…the number of fatal motorcycle crashes began to climb in 1998 and have risen each year since."</em></p> <p>Fell<br /> Op.Cit.</p> <p>The incidence of positive BAC tests is 24% greater in motorcycle vs. car fatalities. [Fell]</p> <p>In a 2004 investigation [MMWR, 2004]:</p> <ul> <li>48% of fatally injured motorcyclists were intoxicated.</li> <li>Peak death rate in this setting shifted from ages 20-24 to ages 40-44.</li> <li>Over age 34, the % of drunken motorcyclists was higher than the % of drunk automobile drivers.</li> </ul> <p>In a recent investigation, 49% of moped operators injured in accidents had a positive blood alcohol level, 45% had a prior DWI and 72% of those were repeat offenders.</p> <p>In addition, 38% had a revoked license at the time of injury and half of these individuals had multiple revocations. [Brintzenhoff]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What are the <span style="color:#FC0;">YELLOW</span> and <span style="color:#900;">RED FLAGS</span> for an increased risk of being an intoxicated driver?</strong></p> <p>Overall, the risk is higher in males and in persons smoking > 1 pack of cigarettes per day.</p> <p>Major risk factors include:</p> <ul> <li>History of substance abuse, including prescription drugs</li> <li>Binge drinking is the #1 predictor; defined as taking 5 or more drinks at one sitting</li> <li>Habitually not wearing seat belt</li> <li>Single vehicle accidents and/or striking fixed vs. moving objects</li> <li>Car crashes occurring between midnight and sunrise</li> <li>Prior convictions for reckless driving, driving too fast for conditions, weaving in and out of traffic, and failure to keep in the proper lane</li> <li>Any criminal history</li> <li>Antisocial personality disorder</li> <li>Borderline personality disorder</li> <li>Multiple current psychiatric disorders, especially mood-related (depression, bipolar disorder, etc.)</li> <li>Frequent/sustained use of benzodiazepines for sleep-related purposes</li> <li>Prior driver's license suspension/revocation</li> <li>"Open container" violations (driving with an open bottle of an alcoholic beverage)</li> <li>Frequently engaging in risk-taking behaviors such as extensive gambling, poor health practices, etc.</li> <li>Delay in reinstatement of suspended/revoked driver's license</li> <li>Elevated MCV on a complete blood count</li> </ul> <p>[Birdsall, Blencowe, Fell, Freeman, Gjerde, Hingson, Pavanello, Romano, Sansone, Schermer, Voss, Wang]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Do many DWI/DUI convicts meet the criteria for an alcohol use disorder?</strong></p> <p>Yes, consistently in study after study:</p> <ul> <li>Miller (1986): 73%</li> <li>Parks (1996): 68%</li> <li>Brinkmann (2002): 48%</li> <li>Furr-Holden (2011): 50%</li> </ul> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is it likely that the % of DWI/DUI convicts diagnosed with an alcohol-related disorder will increase in the future?</strong></p> <p>Yes, because the criteria for this diagnosis have been liberalized substantially in the new edition of the American Psychiatric Association's Diagnostic and Statistical Manual (DSM-5).</p> <p>DSM-5 sets the criteria for psychiatric diagnoses in the United States and is widely used in other countries as well.</p> <p>This said, there has been great controversy over many of the changes embraced in this latest DSM edition and its overall impact remains clouded at this writing.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Which US States have the highest % of drivers involved in fatal crashes with a blood alcohol of 0.08 or higher?</strong></p> <p>In this order [Fell]:</p> <ol> <li>North Dakota</li> <li>South Carolina</li> <li>Montana</li> <li>Wisconsin</li> <li>Rhode Island</li> </ol> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What do we know about DWI/DUI recidivism?</strong></p> <p>Recidivism refers to incurring 2nd and subsequent DWI/DUI convictions.</p> <p><em>"For most individuals, being convicted of a DWI is a life-changing experience most do not want to repeat.</em></p> <p><em>Conversely, individuals with at least two DWI's are at substantial risk of continued episodes of impaired driving either due to substance abuse problems or a denial that he engages in an activity that is harmful to himself or others."</em></p> <p>Danny Perkins, JD, FALU<br /> Self-employed Underwriter/Attorney<br /> On the Risk<br /> 28(2012):38</p> <p><em>"Repeat offenders represent a group that is distinct not only from the general population but also…from the majority of first-time DUI offenders."</em></p> <p>Howard J. Shaffer, et al<br /> Harvard University<br /> Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology<br /> 75(2007):795</p> <ul> <li>The recidivism rate after a first conviction is roughly 10% [Chaudhary]</li> <li>The risk of a 2nd conviction is 10-fold greater than a first conviction; given 2 convictions, the odds of a third are 14-fold higher vs. having just one, and the likelihood continues to rise with additional convictions. [Rauch]</li> <li>The average interval from 1st to 2nd DWI/DUI is 6 years, which raises questions about the value of temporary flat extra premiums in this setting…more on this later. [Cavaiola]</li> <li>The risk of alcohol dependence (alcoholism) is higher in those with more than one DWI conviction whereas the odds of alcohol abuse only (without addiction) actually declines in persons with multiple convictions. [Couture, Shaffer]</li> <li>Recidivists have a high prevalence of other criminality. [Hubicka]</li> <li>Elevations of both GGT and CDT in first-time offenders are associated with a higher risk of recidivism. [Portman]</li> <li>Repeat offenders have a 50% probability of comorbid drug use disorders, over 30% have major depression and at least 15% will be diagnosed with posttraumatic stress disorder. [Albanese, Lapham, Peller]</li> <li>Conduct disorder in childhood and a history of problem gambling at any time are associated with a greater risk of a 2nd DWI/DUI. [LaPlante, Shaffer]</li> <li>Prior traumatic brain injury is significantly more common in recidivists. [Ouimet]</li> </ul> <p>And most comorbid psychiatric diagnoses in DWI/DUI offenders go unrecognized clinically! [McMillan]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is there any clue to identifying the "hard core" drunken driver?</strong></p> <p>Yes – a blood alcohol of 0.15 mg/dL at any time. [Baker, Fell and Tippetts]</p> <p>These are clearly <span style="color:#900;font-weight:bold;">RED FLAG</span> cases.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Is there significant excess mortality in DWI/DUI?</strong></p> <p>Yes.</p> <p>In a Scandinavian study, mortality was 3.8 times expected after full adjustment for risk factors. [Karlsson]</p> <p>Mann found that mortality in DWI recidivists was 2.5-fold higher at ages 15-34, 2-fold greater at ages 35-54 and 1.3 times expected at older ages.</p> <p>For additional mortality information, see the superb <a href="http://www.rgare.com/media-center/Documents/Motor%20Vehicle%20Records%20and%20All-Cause%20Mortality.pdf">MVR study</a> by RGA actuaries Tim Rozar and Scott Rushing.</p> <p>Drunken driving is also substantially implicated in increased odds of hospitalization for a wide range of reasons, including suicide attempts (7.7-fold), drug abuse (16.7-fold) and an alcohol user disorder (16.5-fold). [Karlsson]</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>What are the ideal underwriting resources when assessing applicants with a history of DWI/DUI?</strong></p> <p>Obviously, American underwriters will want a MVR (motor vehicle report) and pharmacy profile (to check for drugs associated with alcoholism and comorbid psychiatric disorders).</p> <p>All applicants should routinely be asked about their driving records on teleinterviews, especially at face amounts where MVRs are not routinely ordered…and in all countries where motor vehicle records cannot be obtained for underwriting purposes!</p> <p>Elevations of the following tests raise a RED FLAG in this setting:</p> <ul> <li>Isolated GGT</li> <li>AST:ALT ratio ≥ 2.0</li> <li>Markedly elevated HDL-C</li> <li>Elevated mean corpuscular volume (MCV) in medical records</li> </ul> <p>In our opinion, all applicants with a history of DWI/DUI within at least 5 – ideally more – years should be required to have a CDT test and if CDT is positive, they should be postponed pending further clinical assessment.</p> <p>We also believe that court records should be routinely checked in these cases given the increased prevalence of criminality.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>Does the use of temporary flat extra premiums for DWI/DUI make sense?</strong></p> <p>In our view, no, because the driver (no pun) of the mortality risk is whether or not the individual has an alcohol use disorder and we use table ratings, not temporary flat extras, for these disorders.</p> <p>One might argue for a temporary flat extra in 1st offense cases provided they are devoid of any other significant clues to possible alcohol abuse. However, the latency interval of 6 years between 1st and 2nd DWI/DUI convictions argues against this approach.</p> <p>In all other cases, a permanent rating – potentially subject to reconsideration after an extended interval free of further issues – makes more sense to us.</p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>We hope you found this paper useful.</strong></p> <p><strong>It is presented in the same format as our narrative type <a href="http://www.hankgeorgeinc.com/Testimonial">Continuing Education Program courses</a>.</strong></p> <p><strong>For more information about these courses, please visit <a href="http://www.hankgeorgeinc.com" title="www.hankgeorgeinc.com">www.hankgeorgeinc.com</a> or contact Esther at esther@hankgeorgeinc.com</strong></p> <p style="padding-top: 2em"><strong>References</strong></p> <p>Albanese. For both groups, associations with health outcomes are different.”</em></p> <p>Boston University epidemiologist R. Curtis Ellison affirms this conclusion, comment that:</p> <p><em>“…almost all studies show a higher risk for CHD (as well as most other diseases and total mortality) for recognized ‘ex-drinkers.’ ”</em></p> <p> </p> <p><strong>Are former drinkers often cigarette smokers</strong>?</p> <p>Numerous studies have shown that robust alcohol consumers are several times more likely to smoke than temperate consumers and lifetime nondrinkers.</p> <p>Data from the British Regional Heart Study demonstrate that those who quit alcohol are ill-disposed to giving up their cigarettes:</p> <p> </p> <table border="1" width="296"> <tbody> <tr> <td height="42" width="126"><strong>Drinking Status</strong></td> <td width="154"><strong>% Current Smokers</strong></td> </tr> <tr> <td>Teetotalers</td> <td>22%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Ex-drinkers</td> <td>39%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Occasional drinkers</td> <td>31%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Light drinkers</td> <td>28%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Moderate drinkers</td> <td>38%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Heavy drinkers</td> <td>48%</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p> </p> <p>We should expect a high prevalence of current tobacco use among applicants claiming to have quit drinking. [Wannamethee]</p> <p> </p> <p><strong>How do ex-drinkers compare to persons in other alcohol use categories in terms of medical impairments?</strong></p> <p><em>“…recent abstainers are more likely to have stopped because of medical concerns.”</em></p> <p>Marc A Schuckit, MD<br /> University of California-San Diego Medical School<br /> Lancet<br /> 373(2009):492</p> <p>Former imbibers are considerably more likely to have significant medical histories, as reflected by these findings from a German study: [Wellmann]</p> <p> </p> <table border="1" height="247" width="362"> <tbody> <tr> <td class="tabletitle" colspan="5"><strong>Alcohol Use Status </strong></td> </tr> <tr> <td width="105"> </td> <td width="52">Never</td> <td width="51">Former</td> <td width="66">Temperate</td> <td width="54">Heavy</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Heart attack</td> <td>10%</td> <td>9%</td> <td>4%</td> <td>2%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Stroke</td> <td>2%</td> <td>4%</td> <td>1%</td> <td>1%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Diabetes</td> <td>5%</td> <td>11%</td> <td>5%</td> <td>3%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Liver disease</td> <td>6%</td> <td>12%</td> <td>3%</td> <td>8%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Stomach disease</td> <td>10%</td> <td>16%</td> <td>7%</td> <td>6%</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p> </p> <p>When gerontologists assessed the relative risks of underlying adversities in older age quitters, they discovered some interesting revelations. [Mukamal]</p> <p>These individuals were distinguished from teetotalers and currently drinking elders by a number of disconcerting revelations, considering that ex-drinkers had:</p> <p>The highest % of diabetics<br /> The highest % with hypertension<br /> The greatest prevalence of depression<br /> The lowest average HDL-C readings<br /> The highest mean fibrinogen levels</p> <p>In addition, older age former alcohol aficionados are 3 times more likely to be disabled than teetotalers and have lower average cognitive scores than persons in all other alcohol use categories. [Lobo]</p> <p>The foregoing evidence strongly suggests that many drinkers do indeed quit consuming alcohol because of illness.</p> <p> </p> <p><strong>Are former alcohol abusers more apt to continue manifesting unfavorable behaviors associated with ongoing alcohol abuse?</strong></p> <p>An investigation by experts in automotive medicine tells us that putative ex-drinkers have numerous risk-taking characteristics similar to those prevalent in current abusive drinkers: [Ryb]</p> <p> </p> <table border="1" height="236" width="328"> <tbody> <tr> <td class="tabletitle" colspan="4"><strong>Alcohol Abuse Status</strong></td> </tr> <tr> <td width="142"> </td> <td width="59">Never</td> <td width="51">Former</td> <td width="72">Current</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Not using seat belts</td> <td>48%</td> <td>61%</td> <td>94%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Speeding for thrills</td> <td>5%</td> <td>15%</td> <td>18%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Single vehicle accidents</td> <td>28%</td> <td>35%</td> <td>61%</td> </tr> <tr> <td>Conviction for assault</td> <td>9%</td> <td>30%</td> <td>36%</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p> </p> <p><strong>How do ex-drinkers fare in terms of mortality?</strong></p> <p>The first studies documenting that mortality based on alcohol consumption was U-shaped (higher in nondrinkers and heavy drinkers; lowest in temperate consumers) did not make a distinction between teetotalers and ex-drinkers. The result was that alcohol abstention appeared to be a high risk scenario. [Klatsky]</p> <p>When these investigators redid their analyses by separating teetotalers from former drinkers, they found that all-cause mortality was indeed favorable in those who never took a drink…</p> <p>…and all of the “abstainer” excess death risk fell squarely upon former users.</p> <p>There have been numerous assessments of mortality sorted by alcohol use category over the intervening years.</p> <p>Let’s look at some of the findings…</p> <p>In a 2003 meta-analysis, Gmel and coworkers contrasted mortality in teetotalers to that of ex-users. They found that aggregate mortality was 44% higher in male former drinkers and 21% greater in their female peers.</p> <p>Dutch epidemiologists followed 16,210 subjects, ages 45 to 70, for 5 years. They learned that mortality was 74% greater in ex-drinkers as compared to teetotalers, and both nonuser groups fared worse than moderate-to-heavy drinkers. [Friesema]</p> <p>Similarly, researchers at the University of Texas Medical School discovered that mortality was 2-fold increased in current nonusers vs. temperate drinkers. Further analysis showed that all of that excess was clustered in the former drinker subset. [Holahan]</p> <p>In a study encompassing over 14,000 adults, 5-year mortality was highest in subjects that went from moderate or heavy use to abstention. There was no increase in the risk of death for individuals than continued to consume 14 or more drinks each week. [Grønbek]</p> <p><em>Bottom line?</em></p> <p>Ex-drinkers have higher mortality than teetotalers, temperate drinkers and in some cases even heavy alcohol users.</p> <p>The loss of the cardioprotective advantages incurred from alcohol consumption cannot explain these outcomes because for this to be true there would be no significant mortality difference in risk between former and never drinkers.</p> <p>Hence, this survival disadvantage associated with discontinuing alcohol use is due to (a) underlying chronic disease and (b) ex-heavy drinkers retaining their other adverse health habits and risk-taking behaviors.</p> <p> </p> <p><strong>How can underwriters deal effectively with the insidious risk associated with quitting drinking?</strong></p> <p>When proposed insureds are asked about alcohol use and they disavow current consumption, ask them if they EVER used alcohol.</p> <p>Then, if they affirm prior indulgence, question them further:</p> <p>When did you quit drinking?</p> <p>Why did you quit drinking?</p> <p><em>Those that cite a specific date for the commencement of abstinence might also be asked what happened the day before!</em></p> <p>If they quit within, say, the last 5 years…and/or medical, occupational, pharmaceutical, legal or driving record issues…it would be prudent to pursue further evidence of insurability.</p> <p>One asset of particular utility in this context is the alcohol marker carbohydrate-deficient transferrin (CDT). If, indeed, they no longer consume alcohol, this test will surely be negative.</p> <p>Attending physicians’ records will also be quite illuminating, especially in cases where substantial prior indulgence has (selectively) clouded their memory!</p> <p> </p> <p> </p> <p><strong>References</strong></p> <p>Ellison.
